  • All For Me Grog
    Traditional sea shanty, first known in the 19th century. A lively folk song about sailors spending their money on alcohol and tobacco.
  • Around The Wild Cape Horn
    Around The Wild Cape Horn was written by Hugh Williams and performed by David Coffin, a renowned American folk musician and sea music specialist. The song is based on a true account by Captain Irving Johnson, who sailed aboard the Peking in 1929 as a young man. Set to an engaging rhythm, the lyrics narrate the harrowing but awe-inspiring journey around Cape Horn. It's often performed with dramatic flair, echoing the tradition of nautical storytelling.
  • Barretts Privateers
    Composed by Stan Rogers, 1976. A modern sea shanty about a privateer’s disastrous voyage.
  • Bay Of Mexico
    Bay of Mexico by Eric Andersen is a folk song written in the 1960s that draws on traditional sea-shanty style. Andersen, part of the Greenwich Village folk scene, reworked the older sailors’ ballad into his own arrangement, preserving its nautical imagery of hardship, adventure, and loss on the seas. His version helped introduce the song to contemporary audiences, blending folk revival sensibilities with traditional maritime themes.
  • Blow The Man Down
    Traditional sea shanty, first known in the 19th century. A spirited sea shanty about life aboard a ship and various maritime adventures.
  • Blow Ye Winds In The Morning
    A traditional sea shanty, this song reflects the harsh life of sailors and their longing for home. Originating in the 19th century, its author is unknown, but it was often sung by sailors during long voyages. It was later preserved in folk music collections.
  • Bones In The Ocean
    Written by David Coffin, 2013. A contemporary sea shanty about loss and remembrance at sea.
  • Cape Ann
    Cape Ann was written by Gordon Bok in the late 1960s and reflects his deep connection to New England maritime life. The song portrays the harsh beauty of the Cape Ann coast and the endurance of those who work the sea. Using spare language and a steady, unadorned melody, it exemplifies Bok’s approach to writing songs that feel rooted in place and tradition.
  • Coast Of High Barbary
    The Coast of High Barbary is a traditional English sea shanty dating back to the 17th century, recounting a naval battle with pirates off the Barbary Coast. It gained popularity among sailors, and its authorship is unknown.
  • Dead Horse
    Traditional American song, first known in the 19th century. A humorous and lively song about a sailor’s encounter with a dead horse.
  • Doodle Let Me Go
    Doodle Let Me Go, also called The Sailor’s Alphabet, is a traditional West Indian sea shanty believed to have originated in the mid-19th century, with no known single author. It was sung in call-and-response form aboard sailing ships, each verse beginning with a letter of the alphabet, often adapted by crews to suit their own humor and experiences while working the rigging.
  • Duna
    Duna was written by Gordon Bok in the early 1970s and draws on Scottish Gaelic language and imagery, reflecting his interest in Celtic tradition and seafaring culture. The song is spare and meditative, focusing on longing, distance, and emotional connection rather than narrative. It has often been mistaken for a traditional Gaelic song because of its modal melody and restrained structure.
  • Farewell To Tarwathie
    Farewell to Tarwathie is a 19th-century Scottish whaling song written by George Scroggie around 1857. Set to the traditional melody of 'Green Bushes,' it reflects a sailor’s farewell to his homeland as he departs for the Greenland whaling grounds. The lyrics evoke the harsh beauty of the Arctic and the loneliness of life at sea. Judy Collins recorded a notable version in 1970 with whale sounds.
  • Good Ship Venus
    The Good Ship Venus is a traditional English bawdy sea song, also widely known as Friggin in the Riggin. Its roots lie in 19th century maritime folk tradition, circulating orally among sailors and in informal songbooks. The song gained wider modern recognition through mid-20th-century folk revival performances and recordings, notably by Cyril Tawney and other British folk singers.
  • Haul Away Joe
    Haul Away Joe is a traditional sea shanty of unknown authorship, widely believed to have originated in the 19th century. It was sung by sailors during work tasks requiring coordinated effort, particularly hauling lines. The song's lyrics often varied, reflecting the improvisational nature of shanties. Its upbeat rhythm and call-and-response format made it popular among maritime crews.
  • Highland Laddie
    Traditional Scottish folk song, first known in the 18th century. Child Ballad 47. A song about a Highland lad and his adventures.
  • Hog-Eye Man
    Hog-Eye Man is a traditional American sea shanty dating back to the mid-19th century, commonly used as a halyard shanty aboard sailing ships. Its raucous lyrics and call-and-response structure made it ideal for coordinated hauling. The term 'hog-eye' likely referred to a flat-bottomed riverboat or, more crudely, to a slang term for dockside workers or women. Its verses were often improvised, bawdy, and humorous, reflecting sailors' lives ashore and afloat.
  • Isle Au Haut Lullabye
    Isle au Haut Lullabye was written by Gordon Bok in the early 1970s and is named for the small island community off the coast of Maine. The song uses the form of a lullaby to evoke safety, continuity, and belonging amid the uncertainties of maritime life. It has become one of Bok’s most widely sung and enduring compositions.
  • Jam On Gerry's Rock
    Jam On Gerry’s Rock is an American folk ballad drawn from 19th-century lumber camp tradition. It tells of a dangerous log jam on a river at a place called Gerry’s Rock, where a riverman named Monroe attempts to break the jam and is killed in the process. The song reflects the hazards of river driving and the fatal consequences of misjudgment in timber work.
  • John Kanaka
    John Kanaka is a traditional sea shanty of Polynesian influence, often sung as a call-and-response work song. Likely dating back to the 19th century, it was used by sailors to keep rhythm while hauling lines. The lyrics feature the repeated phrase 'John Kanaka-nay,' referencing a Hawaiian laborer, though the song's exact origins are uncertain. It remains popular in folk and maritime music circles.
  • Johnny Todd
    Johnny Todd is a traditional Liverpool children’s song and sea shanty dating back to the 19th century, often sung by sailors and dockworkers. The lyrics tell of a young sailor who loses his sweetheart to another man while he is away at sea, reflecting themes of love and betrayal. It was later adapted as the closing theme for the British TV series Z-Cars, giving it lasting cultural recognition.
  • Leave Her, Johnny, Leave Her
    Traditional sea shanty, first known in the 19th century. A song about the end of a ship's voyage and the farewell to a beloved ship.
  • Marco Polo
    Marco Polo is a maritime ballad performed by David Coffin, celebrating the legendary 19th-century clipper ship Marco Polo. Known for its speed and transoceanic voyages, the ship is honored in a chorus repeating 'Marco Polo, fastest on the sea.' The song blends historical tribute with folk tradition and is popular in educational and sea music circles, though the original author is unknown.
  • Mary Ellen Carter
    Mary Ellen Carter was written by Canadian folk singer Stan Rogers and released in 1979 on his album Between the Breaks … Live!. The song tells the inspirational story of a sunken ship, The Mary Ellen Carter, and the determination of her crew to raise her. It's widely praised for its rousing chorus and message of resilience, especially among those facing adversity.
  • Mingulay Boat Song
    Mingulay Boat Song was written in the 1930s by Sir Hugh S. Roberton, founder of the Glasgow Orpheus Choir. Though often mistaken for a traditional Hebridean folk song, the lyrics are Roberton’s, set to the older Gaelic tune 'Creag Ghuanach.' The song evokes fishermen returning home to the Isle of Mingulay, blending romantic imagery with a gentle, rocking rhythm well suited to fingerpicking guitar.
  • Mrs. MacDonald's Lament
    Mrs. Macdonald’s Lament is an original song written and recorded by American folk singer Gordon Bok circa 1969, reflecting on the tough life of a Maine fisherman who keeps going out to sea despite hardship and economic decline. The lyrics portray his persistence and how the next generation follows his example, capturing Bok’s maritime storytelling rooted in New England coastal culture.
  • Northwest Passage
    Written by Stan Rogers, 1981. A folk song about the search for a sea route through the Arctic and the challenges faced by explorers.
  • Old Maui

  • Old Zeb
    Old Zeb is a song by Gordon Bok, written in the late 20th century, portraying an aging Maine fisherman whose life has been shaped by the sea. The song reflects on work, endurance, and the quiet dignity of a man who has outlived many companions, using plain language and maritime imagery to evoke the passing of time and the deep bond between people and place.

  • South Australia
    South Australia is a traditional sea shanty dating from the mid-19th century, sung by sailors as they worked, especially when hauling halyards. Likely originating among British seamen during the height of Australian immigration and trade, the song references voyages to Adelaide and the South Australia region. Its driving rhythm made it well-suited to maritime labor, and it remains popular in folk repertoires worldwide.
